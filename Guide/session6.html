2. The Five Forces Explained

1. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

Question: How intense is the competition between current players?

Example: Coke vs. Pepsi ‚Äî intense advertising wars, price competition

High rivalry = Lower profits

2. Threat of New Entrants

Question: How easy is it for new competitors to enter?

Example: Airlines have high barriers (capital, regulations); software has low barriers

Low barriers = More threat

3. Threat of Substitute Products

Question: Can customers satisfy their needs differently?

Example: Video conferencing (Zoom) substitutes for business travel

More substitutes = Less pricing power

4. Bargaining Power of Buyers

Question: How much leverage do customers have?

Example: Walmart can demand low prices from suppliers due to its massive purchasing volume

Strong buyers = Squeezed margins

5. Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Question: How much leverage do suppliers have?

Example: Microsoft has power over PC makers who need Windows licenses

Strong suppliers = Higher input costs

3. What Makes a Market "Highly Competitive"?

Key Insight

A market is "highly competitive" when ALL five forces are strong. This drives economic profits toward zero.

Highly Competitive = Tough for Firms

  • ‚úì Many fierce rivals
  • ‚úì Easy for new firms to enter
  • ‚úì Lots of substitutes available
  • ‚úì Buyers have strong negotiating power
  • ‚úì Suppliers can demand high prices

Result: Profits squeezed to zero

Attractive Industry = Good for Firms

  • ‚úì Few competitors (or cooperation)
  • ‚úì High barriers to entry
  • ‚úì Few substitutes
  • ‚úì Fragmented buyers (no power)
  • ‚úì Fragmented suppliers

Result: Firms can earn high profits

Session 6 Key Takeaway

Industry attractiveness isn't just about the number of competitors. Use Porter's Five Forces to systematically analyze all sources of competitive pressure. The most profitable industries have weak forces across all five dimensions, creating sustainable profit opportunities.
