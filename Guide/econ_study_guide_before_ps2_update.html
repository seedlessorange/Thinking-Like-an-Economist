Price Elasticity of Demand

Elasticity Formula

Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price:

$$E = \left(\frac{P}{Q}\right) \times \left(\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P}\right)$$

Where:

  • P = current price
  • Q = current quantity
  • ŒîQ = change in quantity
  • ŒîP = change in price
Inelastic Demand

|E| < 1

Quantity is less responsive to price changes

  • Graph: Steep demand curve
  • Examples: Medicine, gasoline, necessities
  • Strategy: Can raise prices without losing many customers
Elastic Demand

|E| > 1

Quantity is more responsive to price changes

  • Graph: Flat demand curve
  • Examples: Luxury goods, goods with many substitutes
  • Strategy: Lower prices to gain market share

Worked Example: Finding the Demand Curve

1
Given Information

A linear demand curve has the form: Q = a - bP

We know two facts:

  • At price P = 10, quantity demanded Q = 100
  • At this point, elasticity E = -2
2
Find the slope (b)

From the elasticity formula: $E = \frac{P}{Q} \times \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P}$

For a linear demand Q = a - bP, the slope is: $\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} = -b$

Substituting: $-2 = \frac{10}{100} \times (-b)$

Solving: $-2 = -0.1b$ ‚Üí b = 20

3
Find the intercept (a)

Use the point (P=10, Q=100) in the equation Q = a - bP:

$100 = a - 20(10)$

$100 = a - 200$

a = 300

4
Final Demand Curve

Q = 300 - 20P

Answer This demand curve passes through (P=10, Q=100) with elasticity E = -2

Worked Example: Finding Equilibrium

Given demand Qd = a - bP and supply Qs = c + dP, find equilibrium:

1
Set Demand = Supply

At equilibrium: Qd = Qs

$a - bP^* = c + dP^*$

2
Solve for P*

$a - c = bP^* + dP^*$

$a - c = (b + d)P^*$

$$P^* = \frac{a - c}{b + d}$$

3
Solve for Q*

Substitute P* back into either demand or supply equation:

$$Q^* = a - b \times \frac{a - c}{b + d} = \frac{ad + bc}{b + d}$$

Numerical Example: If Qd = 300 - 20P and Qs = -100 + 10P
  • a = 300, b = 20, c = -100, d = 10
  • P* = (300 - (-100))/(20 + 10) = 400/30 = 13.33
  • Q* = 300 - 20(13.33) = 33.4

Gains from Trade

  • Consumer Surplus (CS): The area below the demand curve but above the equilibrium price (P*). This represents the value consumers get above what they paid.
  • Producer Surplus (PS): The area above the supply curve but below the equilibrium price (P*). This represents the profit producers make above their cost to produce.

Economists generally favor "free markets" because they maximize the total gains from trade (CS + PS).

Market Interventions

Sometimes, an actor (like the government) intervenes in the market.

Price Minimum (Pmin)

A price (like a minimum wage) set above the equilibrium P*.

Result: Causes an Excess (Surplus), because supply (Qs) will be greater than demand (Qd).

Price Maximum (Pmax)

A price (like rent control) set below the equilibrium P*.

Result: Causes a Shortage, because demand (Qd) will be greater than supply (Qs).

Interventions like price caps or floors "artificially limit" the gains from trade by creating shortages or excesses. However, the political motive for such interventions (e.g., protecting consumers or an industry) may be legitimate.

1. Case Study: Globalization & Free Trade (Imports)

This case analyzes what happens when a country opens to free trade and the world price (Pw) is lower than the domestic price (Pa).

Gains from Trade (Imports)

  • The price drops from Pa (Autarky/Domestic) to Pw (World).
  • Consumers win BIG: Their surplus expands significantly. They get to buy more (Qd) for a lower price.
  • Domestic Producers lose: Their surplus shrinks. They sell less (Qs) for a lower price.
  • Net Result: The economy as a whole gains. The gains to consumers are larger than the losses to producers. The difference (Qd - Qs) is imported.

Intervention: Tariffs

A tariff is a tax on imports, raising the price from Pw to Pt (Tariff Price). This "protects" domestic producers but hurts consumers.

  • Winners: Domestic producers (get higher price Pt) and the government (collects tax revenue).
  • Losers: Consumers (must pay higher price Pt and buy less).
  • Net Result: The economy as a whole loses. The "deadweight loss" triangles represent value that is destroyed and lost to everyone.

2. Case Study: Electricity Markets (Merit Order)

In a competitive "merit order" market, the cheapest unit produces first, then the next cheapest, until demand is met. The market price is set by the last and most expensive unit needed to satisfy demand.

The Generic Principle of Supply (Merit Order)

How it works:

  1. The most cost-competitive unit produces at full capacity (if capacity < total demand)
  2. The second most cost-competitive unit does the same (if combined capacity still < total demand)
  3. This continues until total demand is satisfied
  4. Units with higher production costs that aren't needed remain idle, shut down, or aren't built

Paper Industry Example

Total Demand = 75,000 tons.

  • Alpha 1 (Cost ‚Ç¨700) produces 40,000 tons.
  • Bravo 1 (Cost ‚Ç¨750) produces 25,000 tons. (Total = 65,000)
  • Alpha 2 (Cost ‚Ç¨850) produces the final 10,000 tons. (Total = 75,000)

Result: The market price is ‚Ç¨850 (set by Alpha 2). All producers (Alpha 1, Bravo 1, and Alpha 2) get paid ‚Ç¨850 for every ton.

Strategic Questions:

  • Should Bravo increase B1 capacity? YES - Good idea. Bravo 1 is competitive at ‚Ç¨750, below the market price of ‚Ç¨850.
  • Should Alpha close A2? NO - Bad idea. Alpha 2 is needed to meet demand and sets the market price. Even though it's the most expensive, it's still profitable at ‚Ç¨850.

EU Electricity Market Design

The EU applies this merit order system to electricity, stacking power sources from cheapest to most expensive:

  1. Renewables (lowest marginal cost)
  2. Nuclear (constant power stream)
  3. Coal
  4. Natural Gas (quick to fire up)
  5. Oil (highest cost, sets price when needed)

Rationale: This design provides information for optimal resource allocation. Marginal cost pricing signals the value of additional electricity units and allows quick adjustments across different time horizons.

The Controversy

The Problem: When expensive sources (like gas) are needed, all producers receive that high price. This creates windfall profits for low-cost producers (renewables, nuclear).

From The Economist (Sept 8, 2022): "The idea behind this is straightforward. To generate electricity at low cost and in an increasingly carbon-free manner requires a lot of information. Some technologies, such as nuclear, are good at providing a constant stream of power. Gas is good for firing up quickly. Solar is best around lunchtime. The marginal cost of power provides the information needed to switch between them, by signaling the value of an additional unit of electricity."

The Crisis: The system wasn't built for crisis situations like the Russian gas cutoff. This raised questions:

  • Geopolitical: Where to source alternatives to Russian gas?
  • Economic: Subsidies are illegal in the EU, but does this force acceptance of redistributed comparative advantages?
  • Industrial: Growing electricity price differences may have long-term manufacturing effects

International Electricity Price Comparison (2021 Industry Prices)

Price per kilowatt-hour (pence):

  • United Kingdom: ~14 (highest)
  • France: ~11
  • Portugal: ~11
  • Germany: ~10
  • United States: ~6.25 (lowest)

Key Insight: Significant tax components in EU prices create competitive disadvantages for European manufacturers. This creates real industrial, political, and economic challenges for the EU.

3. Case Study: Pricing Strategy (Fixed Supply)

When supply is fixed (like seats in a stadium), the supply curve is a vertical line. The only thing that changes is the price, which determines how many tickets are sold (or not sold).

  • Price P‚ÇÅ (Equilibrium): The "sell-out" price. Demand exactly meets supply. Maximizes revenue.
  • Price P‚ÇÉ (Premium): Set above equilibrium. You sell fewer tickets (Qd at P‚ÇÉ) but build a premium brand image.
  • Price P‚ÇÇ (Buzz): Set below equilibrium. Creates a shortage (Qd at P‚ÇÇ > Q*). You "lose" potential revenue, but the shortage creates a "buzz" and acts as powerful advertising.

4. Case Study: Labor & Housing Markets

Minimum Wage (Labor Market)

In the labor market, workers are the suppliers and firms are the demanders. A minimum wage is a Price Floor set above the equilibrium wage.

Arguments FOR Minimum Wage:

  • Human reasons: Ensuring decent living standards for workers
  • Productivity problem: Higher wages may increase worker productivity and motivation
  • Insider/Outsider theory: Protects employed workers from unfair competition
  • Macroeconomic issue: Maintains aggregate demand by ensuring consumers have purchasing power
  • Political motivation: Popular policy measure with broad public support

Arguments AGAINST Minimum Wage:

Microeconomic Theory Prediction: Creates unemployment (gap between labor supplied and demanded at W_min).

  • At the higher wage W_min, more people want to work (Qs on supply curve)
  • But fewer jobs are available (Qd on demand curve)
  • Results in unemployment of Qs - Qd
  • The surplus of labor means some willing workers cannot find jobs

International Context: Minimum Wages as % of Median Monthly Earnings (2018)

Highest:

  • France: 66%
  • Portugal: 64%
  • Slovenia: 62%

Lowest:

  • Estonia: 42%
  • Malta: 43%
  • Spain: 44%

Key Insight: The "thickness" of the market (how binding the minimum wage is relative to median earnings) varies significantly across countries, creating different labor market outcomes.

Rent Control (Housing Market)

Rent control is a Price Ceiling set below the equilibrium rent. This creates a Shortage of housing, as more people want to rent (Qd) than there are apartments available (Qs). This can lead to disrepair and a black market.

Policy Failure Example: Zero-Interest Rate Subsidies

Government Objective: Help households buy houses (reduce prices)

The Policy: Offer zero-interest loans to make buying easier

What Actually Happened:

  1. Zero-interest loans make buying easier ‚Üí Demand curve shifts RIGHT
  2. Housing supply is fixed in the short run (vertical supply curve)
  3. Price increases from P* to P1
  4. Government worsened the problem by making prices higher!

Critical Lesson: Failed to identify the correct lever. The problem was supply-side (not enough housing), but the policy targeted the demand-side. To reduce prices, need to increase housing supply, not stimulate demand.

5. Case Study: The "War on Drugs" (Inelastic Demand)

When demand is inelastic (a steep or vertical line), consumers will buy the same amount (Q*) regardless of price. This is common with addictive goods where consumers need the product regardless of cost.

US Government Policy: Destroy Latin American Drug Crops

Intended Effect: Reduce drug availability and harm dealers

Actual Effect:

  1. Supply curve shifts LEFT (reduced supply from crop destruction)
  2. With vertical demand (addicts need drugs regardless of price), quantity consumed stays at Q*
  3. Price increases dramatically from P1 to P2
  4. Market becomes MORE attractive to remaining producers
  5. Empowers remaining drug dealers with higher profits at same volume

Unintended Consequence & Better Strategy

If the government targets suppliers (shifting Supply Left), the quantity consumed does not change. The only result is that the price increases dramatically. This makes the drug market more profitable for remaining dealers, undermining the policy's goal.

Better Strategy: Target the demand side (domestic consumption, treatment programs, education) rather than supply. When demand is inelastic, supply-side interventions are counterproductive.

Critical Lesson: Another example of targeting the wrong lever. With inelastic demand, reducing supply enriches remaining suppliers without reducing consumption.

üí∞ Session 4: Pricing Strategies

This session explores how firms use microeconomic principles to set prices, focusing on price discrimination and elasticity to extract more economic value from consumers.

Core Concept: Gaining Pricing Power

Fundamental Question: How can firms use their pricing strategies and market power to guarantee a greater chunk of economic value for themselves?

Two Basic Approaches:

  1. Price Discrimination - Extracting consumer surplus by charging different prices to different consumers
  2. Price Elasticity - Harnessing different consumer reactions to price changes

Understanding Consumer Surplus

What is Consumer Surplus?

  • The upper triangular area above the equilibrium price on a supply-demand graph
  • Represents the aggregate sum of money consumers would have been willing to spend but didn't have to because market forces set the price lower than their maximum willingness to pay
  • The demand curve is just a mean/total‚Äîit doesn't display the diversity of demand
  • It's actually the addition of all individual demand curves present in the market

Key Insight: This consumer surplus is money that businesses can target through price discrimination, since consumers were willing to spend it!

1. Strategy 1: Price Discrimination

The goal is to capture more Consumer Surplus (the "money left on the table"). By discriminating between different kinds of consumers, firms can extract a greater chunk of this surplus. All strategies targeting this surplus revolve around price discrimination, though discrimination varies in degrees.

Three Degrees of Price Discrimination

First-Degree Price Discrimination (Perfect Discrimination)

Concept: Charging each customer their exact maximum willingness to pay

Examples:

  • Airline Upgrade Auctions: Air Mauritius introduced "UpgradeNow," allowing passengers to bid for premium cabin seats
  • Airlines like Air New Zealand, Brussels Airlines, Copa Airlines, Czech Airlines, El Al, Etihad, TAP Portugal, and Virgin Atlantic allow bidding for business class
  • Why it works: Fixed costs remain almost unchanged regardless of seat occupancy, making this strategy highly profitable
Second-Degree Price Discrimination (Non-linear Pricing)

Concept: Price varies based on quantity purchased or self-selection, but all customers face the same price schedule

Examples:

  • Flat rates independent of consumption amount (amusement park all-day passes)
  • "Ladies' night" at clubs (gender-based pricing)
  • Quantity discounts (bulk purchases)
  • Different product versions (economy vs. business class with same schedule)
Third-Degree Price Discrimination (Market Segmentation)

Concept: Segmenting markets based on observable characteristics and charging different prices to each segment

Examples:

  • Geo-marketing: Charging different prices based on location/country
  • Student/senior citizen discounts
  • Business vs. leisure travel pricing
  • Regional pricing for software and digital goods

2. Strategy 2: Price Elasticity

The Problem with Cost-Plus Pricing: Uncle Guy's Pizza

Uncle Guy's pricing strategy:

  1. Calculate production cost (ingredients, etc.)
  2. Add a 20% markup
  3. This becomes the price

Problem with this approach: It completely ignores how consumers actually respond to price changes! A smart strategy uses elasticity, not just a simple cost-plus markup.

Understanding Price Elasticity

Definition: Elasticity measures how responsive demand is to price changes.

The Slope of the Demand Curve:

  • A steeper slope = demand is LESS reactive to price (relatively inelastic)
  • A flatter slope = demand is MORE reactive to price (relatively elastic)

Key Principle: Demand reacts to changes in price in general, but proportions vary across:

  • Different products (medicine vs. luxury goods)
  • Different consumers (price-sensitive vs. brand-loyal)
  • Different times (short-run vs. long-run; product lifecycle stages)

Important Note: Elasticity changes at each point along the curve‚Äîit's not constant!

Comparing Elastic and Inelastic Demand

  • Inelastic Demand (Steep Curve - "D"): Demand is less reactive to price changes. Consumers will buy even if the price goes up. (e.g., medicine, gasoline, insulin).
  • Elastic Demand (Flatter Curve - "d"): Demand is more reactive to price changes. A small price drop can lead to a large increase in demand. (e.g., specific brand of soda, airline tickets, restaurant meals).

A price drop from P‚ÇÅ to P‚ÇÇ triggers:

  • A greater increase in quantity demanded for the elastic curve (d)
  • A smaller increase in quantity demanded for the inelastic curve (D)

Practical Application: Buy One, Get One Free (BOGO)

  • What it is: Effectively a 50% price reduction for consumers buying two units
  • Who it targets: Price-sensitive consumers who respond strongly to price changes
  • When it works: When demand is relatively elastic - consumers will significantly increase purchases in response to the price drop
  • Strategy: Stimulate volume by making price reductions attractive to elastic segments

Dynamic Pricing: Elasticity Changes Over Time

The Product Life Cycle Connection

Pricing strategies should evolve as elasticity changes throughout a product's life:

1. Introduction Phase
  • Low sales volume
  • High consumer uncertainty
  • Elasticity varies by strategy
2. Growth Phase
  • Rapidly increasing sales
  • Market awareness builds
  • Demand becomes less elastic
3. Maturity Phase
  • Peak sales
  • Market saturation
  • More competition ‚Üí more elastic
4. Decline Phase
  • Falling sales
  • Increased competition
  • Highly elastic demand

Case Study: Dynamic Pricing & Product Lifecycle (iPhone 2007)

The Strategy

  1. Introduction (Price Skimming): Launched at $600. This targets early adopters who have very inelastic demand. They are willing to pay a premium to be first.
  2. The Pivot (Targeting Elasticity): Price cut to $400 just two months later. This was a strategic pivot to target the mainstream market, which has a much more elastic demand and is more price-sensitive.

The Backlash: The early adopters (most loyal customers) were furious, feeling used. Apple had to offer a $100 store credit to appease them, acknowledging the "bumpy" nature of this strategy.

Trade-offs:

  • Lost: Empty seats (Q‚ÇÉ < Q*) - foregone ticket revenue
  • Gained: Premium revenue (P‚ÇÉ - P‚ÇÅ) √ó Q‚ÇÉ on tickets sold
  • Gained: Exclusivity and scarcity value
  • Gained: Enhanced brand prestige - "You are the new talk in town"
  • Gained: Opportunity to monetize through merchandise and future sales

Key Insight: "It creates a place where not everyone can do this" - The exclusivity itself becomes valuable. Creates HYPE and premium brand positioning. Consumer surplus grows in intangible ways (prestige, status) that can be captured through merchandise and future sales.

"Intangible - never in your pocket" but builds long-term brand value.

Strategy 2: Volume & Merchandise (Paul McCartney Approach)

Scenario: Set price P‚ÇÇ < P‚ÇÅ (below sellout price)

Trade-offs:

  • Lost: Ticket revenue per seat (P‚ÇÅ - P‚ÇÇ) per ticket
  • Gained: More attendees (Q‚ÇÇ > Q*) - fuller venues, more energy
  • Gained: Increased consumer surplus for fans
  • Gained: Higher merchandise sales (more people = more merch)
  • Gained: Enhanced brand accessibility and goodwill

Key Insight: "P‚ÇÇ makes sense if you're Paul McCartney and bring more people to your concerts" - Lower concert prices increase consumer surplus, which can be captured through merchandise sales and brand loyalty. The strategy: Pass surplus to fans on tickets, extract it back through merchandise.

Strategic Decision Framework:

The optimal pricing strategy depends on:

  • Brand positioning: Are you building exclusivity or accessibility?
  • Revenue streams: Do you have strong merchandise/ancillary revenue?
  • Market context: Are you building a brand or at peak popularity?
  • Long-term goals: Maximizing current profit vs. building future value

Case Study: New Cereal Brand - Opposite Strategy

Contrast the iPhone's price skimming approach with a new cereal brand entering a crowded market:

Launch Strategy: Low Introductory Price

Market Conditions:

  • Consumers uncertain about quality
  • Many substitutes available (other cereal brands)
  • Need to incentivize trial
  • Demand is highly elastic at launch

Strategy: Launch at LOW price to encourage trial and build market share

Growth Strategy: Gradually Raise Prices

As Brand Develops:

  • Brand loyalty develops
  • Consumers develop preferences and habits
  • Switching costs increase (habit formation)
  • Demand becomes less elastic

Strategy: Can raise prices as demand becomes less elastic and brand loyalty builds

Key Difference from iPhone:

  • iPhone: High price ‚Üí Lower price (price skimming for innovative product)
  • Cereal: Low price ‚Üí Higher price (penetration pricing for commodity market)

The optimal dynamic pricing strategy depends on your product's uniqueness, competitive environment, and how consumer elasticity evolves.

  1. Consumer Surplus Represents Opportunity: The gap between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay is a target for firms to capture through discrimination strategies.
  2. Not All Consumers React the Same: Understanding elasticity differences across consumer segments, products, and time periods is crucial for optimal pricing.
  3. Cost-Plus Pricing is Insufficient: Simply adding a markup to costs ignores demand-side factors and consumer price sensitivity. Uncle Guy's approach leaves money on the table.
  4. Dynamic Pricing is Essential: Optimal prices change over time as market conditions, competition, and consumer elasticity evolve through the product lifecycle.
  5. Price Discrimination Requires Market Power: Firms must have some degree of market power to successfully implement discrimination strategies. Perfect competition eliminates this opportunity.
  6. Multiple Strategies Can Coexist: Firms often combine discrimination and elasticity-based approaches simultaneously (e.g., airline pricing uses both auctions and dynamic pricing).
  7. Elasticity is Context-Dependent: The same product can have different elasticities for different consumers (early adopters vs. mainstream) and different times (short-run vs. long-run).

The "Run, But How?" Question

Having market power and understanding pricing theory is just the beginning‚Äîthe challenge is knowing which specific strategy to implement given your particular market conditions, product life cycle stage, and competitive environment.

Like Alice in Wonderland running through the maze, firms must navigate complex decisions about when to skim vs. penetrate, when to discriminate vs. uniform pricing, and how to respond to changing elasticity over time.

Economies of Scale and Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

Understanding How Costs Change with Scale

As firms produce more units, their costs per unit often change dramatically. Understanding this relationship is crucial for determining optimal firm size and competitive strategy.

Economies of Scale

As production volume increases, average costs decrease. This happens for several reasons:

üè≠
Spreading Fixed Costs

Factory rent, machinery, R&D costs are spread over more units ‚Üí Lower cost per unit

üìö
Learning by Doing

Workers become more efficient with experience, processes improve, fewer mistakes

üîß
Task Specialization

Division of labor allows workers to focus on specific tasks ‚Üí Higher productivity

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

MES is the lowest production volume at which a firm achieves minimum average cost. This defines the optimal scale and size for a firm in a given industry.

Below MES: Firm is operating inefficiently - costs are still decreasing

At MES: Firm reaches optimal efficiency - lowest average cost

Above MES: May experience diseconomies of scale - coordination costs, bureaucracy, management complexity

Strategic Implications:

  • High MES industries (automobiles, aircraft): Few large firms can compete ‚Üí Natural oligopoly
  • Low MES industries (restaurants, retail): Many small firms can compete efficiently ‚Üí More competitive markets
  • New entrants must reach MES quickly to survive against established competitors
  • Learning by doing and task specialization lead a firm to become efficient - but only up to MES

Example: Automobile Manufacturing

High MES: A car factory needs to produce ~200,000 units/year to reach minimum efficient scale

  • Below 200k: High costs per vehicle - can't compete
  • At 200k: Achieves competitive cost structure
  • This is why you see relatively few auto manufacturers globally
  • New entrants (Tesla) must rapidly scale to MES or face cost disadvantage

Diseconomies of Scale:

After MES, firms may experience increasing average costs due to:

  • Management coordination challenges
  • Communication breakdowns in large organizations
  • Bureaucratic inefficiencies
  • Loss of flexibility and innovation

This is why some industries have optimal firm sizes - getting too big can be counterproductive!
