Core Concepts

1. Case Study: Globalization & Free Trade (Imports)

This case analyzes what happens when a country opens to free trade and the world price (Pw) is lower than the domestic price (Pa).

Gains from Trade (Imports)

  • The price drops from Pa (Autarky/Domestic) to Pw (World).
  • Consumers win BIG: Their surplus expands significantly. They get to buy more (Qd) for a lower price.
  • Domestic Producers lose: Their surplus shrinks. They sell less (Qs) for a lower price.
  • Net Result: The economy as a whole gains. The gains to consumers are larger than the losses to producers. The difference (Qd - Qs) is imported.

Intervention: Tariffs

A tariff is a tax on imports, raising the price from Pw to Pt (Tariff Price). This "protects" domestic producers but hurts consumers.

  • Winners: Domestic producers (get higher price Pt) and the government (collects tax revenue).
  • Losers: Consumers (must pay higher price Pt and buy less).
  • Net Result: The economy as a whole loses. The "deadweight loss" triangles represent value that is destroyed and lost to everyone.
2. Case Study: Electricity Markets (Merit Order)

In a competitive "merit order" market, the cheapest unit produces first, then the next cheapest, until demand is met. The market price is set by the last and most expensive unit needed to satisfy demand.

Paper Industry Example

Total Demand = 75,000 tons.

  • Alpha 1 (Cost 700) produces 40,000 tons.
  • Bravo 1 (Cost 750) produces 25,000 tons. (Total = 65,000)
  • Alpha 2 (Cost 850) produces the final 10,000 tons. (Total = 75,000)

Result: The market price is 850 (set by Alpha 2). All producers (Alpha 1, Bravo 1, and Alpha 2) get paid 850 for every ton.

Key Insight: Marginal Unit Sets Price
  • In merit-order markets, all units receive the same market price
  • The price equals the cost of the marginal (last needed) producer
  • Inframarginal producers (lower cost) earn significant profits (producer surplus)
  • The marginal producer breaks even (zero economic profit)
3. Case Study: Pricing Strategy (Fixed Supply)

When supply is fixed (like seats in a stadium), the supply curve is a vertical line. The price determines how many tickets are sold (or not sold).

Three Pricing Strategies

  • Price P‚ÇÅ (Equilibrium): The "sell-out" price. Demand exactly meets supply. Maximizes revenue.
  • Price P‚ÇÉ (Premium): Set above equilibrium. You sell fewer tickets (Qd at P‚ÇÉ) but build a premium brand image.
  • Price P‚ÇÇ (Buzz): Set below equilibrium. Creates a shortage (Qd at P‚ÇÇ > Q*). You "lose" potential revenue, but the shortage creates a "buzz" and acts as powerful advertising.
4. Case Study: Labor & Housing Markets

Minimum Wage (Labor Market)

In the labor market, workers are the suppliers and firms are the demanders. A minimum wage is a Price Floor set above the equilibrium wage. This creates a Surplus of labor (Unemployment), as Qs > Qd.

Rent Control (Housing Market)

Rent control is a Price Ceiling set below the equilibrium rent. This creates a Shortage of housing, as more people want to rent (Qd) than there are apartments available (Qs). This can lead to disrepair and a black market.

5. Case Study: The "War on Drugs" (Inelastic Demand)

When demand is inelastic (a steep or vertical line), consumers will buy the same amount (Q*) regardless of price. This is common with addictive goods.

Unintended Consequence

If the government targets suppliers (shifting Supply Left), the quantity consumed does not change. The only result is that the price increases dramatically. This makes the drug market more profitable for remaining dealers, undermining the policy's goal.

6. Calculating Consumer & Producer Surplus

Surplus Formulas

Consumer Surplus (CS): Area below demand curve and above price

$$CS = \frac{1}{2} \times Q^* \times (P_{\text{max}} - P^*)$$

Producer Surplus (PS): Area above supply curve and below price

$$PS = \frac{1}{2} \times Q^* \times (P^* - P_{\text{min}})$$

Total Surplus:

$$TS = CS + PS$$

When the country opens to trade with cheaper foreign shoes
Answer
Shoe Market After Opening to Trade

Analysis:

  • World price (PW) < Autarky price (PA): Foreign shoes are cheaper
  • At PW:
    • Domestic quantity demanded: Qd (increases from QA)
    • Domestic quantity supplied: Qs (decreases from QA)

3
Is trade good or bad for the economy? (Hint: Think in terms of surplus)
Answer

Trade is GOOD for overall welfare.

Gains from Trade Analysis

Surplus Changes:

  • Consumer Surplus: A ‚Üí A + B + D (INCREASES)
  • Producer Surplus: B + C ‚Üí C (DECREASES)
  • Total Surplus: A + B + C ‚Üí A + B + C + D
  • Net Gain from Trade: D (deadweight gain)
Key Insight: Trade Creates Winners and Losers
  • Winners: Consumers gain B + D
  • Losers: Domestic producers lose B
  • Net effect: Gains (D) represent net welfare improvement
  • In principle, winners could compensate losers and still be better off
4
Why is trade hard to sell politically?
Answer

The Political Economy Problem:

  • Gains are diffuse: Millions of consumers each gain a small amount (D distributed across many people)
  • Losses are concentrated: Small number of domestic producers lose B
  • Collective action problem:
    • Producers: Few in number, large losses each ‚Üí Easy to organize lobbying
    • Consumers: Many, small gains each ‚Üí Hard to organize politically
  • Visibility: Job losses are visible and newsworthy; cheaper prices are taken for granted

Example: US Steel Tariffs

When the US imposes steel tariffs:

  • ~140,000 steel workers benefit (visible, concentrated)
  • ~320 million consumers pay slightly higher prices for cars, appliances, etc. (invisible, diffuse)
  • Steel workers lobby effectively; consumers don't organize
  • Result: Politically popular despite reducing total welfare
5
How to make trade easier to sell politically?
Answer

Policy Options:

  1. Tariffs (gradual opening):
    • Temporary tariff that decreases over time
    • Gives domestic industry time to adjust
    • Still captures most gains from trade eventually
  2. Improve competitiveness:
    • Reduce costs (e.g., lower taxes, energy costs)
    • Increase productivity (better technology, training)
    • Shifts domestic supply curve right
  3. Improve branding/quality:
    • Differentiate from foreign goods
    • Shift to higher-quality segments
    • Example: Italian shoes vs. Chinese shoes
  4. Transition assistance:
    • Use tariff revenue or tax revenue to help displaced workers
    • Retraining programs
    • Unemployment insurance
Exercise 3: Industry Cost Curve (McKinsey Article)

Context: Paper industry with demand of 75,000 tonnes. Three firms (Alpha, Bravo, Romeo) have production units with different costs and capacities.

Firm Unit Cost per Tonne ($) Capacity (tonnes)
Alpha Alpha 1 700 40,000
Alpha Alpha 2 850 25,000
Bravo Bravo 1 750 25,000
Bravo Bravo 2 1200 100,000
Romeo Romeo 1 1000 10,000
1
Which units will produce? What is the market price?
Answer

Merit-Order Dispatch Logic: Units produce in order of cost until demand is met.

Industry Cost Curve (Merit Order)

Production Order:

  1. Alpha 1: Cost $700, Capacity 40,000 ‚Üí Cumulative: 40,000 tonnes
  2. Bravo 1: Cost $750, Capacity 25,000 ‚Üí Cumulative: 65,000 tonnes
  3. Alpha 2: Cost $850, Capacity 25,000 ‚Üí Cumulative: 90,000 tonnes

Since demand = 75,000 tonnes:

  • Alpha 1 produces full capacity: 40,000 tonnes
  • Bravo 1 produces full capacity: 25,000 tonnes
  • Alpha 2 produces partial: 10,000 out of 25,000 capacity
  • Market price = $1,000 (cost of next available entrant = Romeo 1)
Price-Setting Logic

In competitive markets, price = marginal cost of the most expensive unit needed to meet demand.

Here, Alpha 2 is the marginal unit. It could charge up to $1,000 (Romeo's cost) without Romeo undercutting it.

3
What if Bravo increases capacity of Bravo 1?
Answer

Strategic Problem for Bravo:

If Bravo 1 capacity increases from 25,000 to 35,000+ tonnes:

  • Total production: Alpha 1 (40,000) + Bravo 1 (35,000) = 75,000 tonnes
  • Alpha 2 no longer needed ‚Üí Exits market
  • New market price = $850 (Alpha 2's cost)
Effect of Bravo Capacity Expansion

Impact on Bravo 1:

  • Before: 25,000 tonnes √ó ($1,000 - $750) = $6,250,000 profit
  • After: 35,000 tonnes √ó ($850 - $750) = $3,500,000 profit
  • Net effect: LOSS of $2,750,000!

Capacity Paradox

Bravo should NOT expand capacity because:

  • Expansion pushes out Alpha 2 (the marginal unit)
  • This lowers market price from $1,000 to $850
  • The price decrease hurts Bravo MORE than the volume increase helps
  • This is why firms sometimes deliberately limit capacity!
4
Should Alpha close Alpha 2?
Answer

Strategic Consideration for Alpha:

If Alpha closes Alpha 2:

  • Production: Alpha 1 (40,000) + Bravo 1 (25,000) = 65,000 tonnes
  • Remaining demand: 10,000 tonnes
  • Romeo 1 becomes marginal unit
  • New market price = $1,200 (Bravo 2's cost, since Romeo only has 10k capacity)

Impact on Alpha 1:

  • Before closing Alpha 2:
    • Alpha 1 profit: 40,000 √ó ($1,000 - $700) = $12,000,000
    • Alpha 2 profit: 10,000 √ó ($1,000 - $850) = $1,500,000
    • Total Alpha profit: $13,500,000
  • After closing Alpha 2:
    • Alpha 1 profit: 40,000 √ó ($1,200 - $700) = $20,000,000
    • Alpha 2 profit: $0
    • Total Alpha profit: $20,000,000
  • Net gain from closing: $6,500,000!
Strategic Capacity Withholding

Alpha SHOULD close Alpha 2 because:

  • Sacrificing 10,000 tonnes at marginal profit ($1,500,000)
  • Gains $8,000,000 on Alpha 1's 40,000 tonnes (price increase of $200)
  • Net gain: $6,500,000
  • This shows how market power can lead to capacity withholding to raise prices
