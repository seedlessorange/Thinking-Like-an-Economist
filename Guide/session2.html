Core Concepts

1. The Demand Curve

The demand curve shows the relationship between price and quantity demanded, holding all other factors constant (ceteris paribus).

Demand Function

$$Q_d = f(P, I, P_s, P_c, T, E)$$

Where: Qd = quantity demanded, P = price, I = income, Ps = price of substitutes, Pc = price of complements, T = tastes, E = expectations

Law of Demand

As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship).

  • Why? Higher prices ‚Üí Lower purchasing power, Substitution effect
  • Slope: Downward sloping (negative slope)
  • Movement vs. Shift: Price change = movement ALONG curve; Other factors = SHIFT of curve
2. The Supply Curve

The supply curve shows the relationship between price and quantity supplied.

Supply Function

$$Q_s = g(P, C, T, E)$$

Where: Qs = quantity supplied, P = price, C = costs (inputs, technology), T = taxes/subsidies, E = expectations

Law of Supply

As price increases, quantity supplied increases (positive relationship).

  • Why? Higher prices ‚Üí More profitable to produce
  • Slope: Upward sloping (positive slope)
  • Short run vs. Long run: Supply more elastic in long run (more time to adjust)
3. Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where supply equals demand. At this point, there is no tendency for price or quantity to change.

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium Condition

$$Q_d = Q_s$$

At equilibrium price P*, quantity demanded equals quantity supplied (Q*)

Key Properties of Equilibrium
  • Market clearing: No shortage or surplus
  • Stability: If price ‚â† P*, market forces push toward P*
  • Efficiency: Maximizes total surplus (assuming no externalities)
  • Dynamic: Equilibrium changes when supply or demand shifts
4. Comparative Statics

Analysis of how equilibrium changes when underlying conditions change.

Comparative Statics: Demand Increase

Demand Shifts (Rightward)

Causes:

  • Income increases (normal good)
  • Price of substitute increases
  • Price of complement decreases
  • Tastes change in favor of good
  • Expectations of future price increase

Effect: Price ‚Üë, Quantity ‚Üë

Comparative Statics: Supply Decrease

Supply Shifts (Leftward)

Causes:

  • Input costs increase
  • Technology worsens (rare)
  • Taxes increase or subsidies decrease
  • Natural disaster
  • Expectations of future price increase

Effect: Price ‚Üë, Quantity ‚Üì

Double Shifts

When both supply and demand shift simultaneously:

  • If both shift right: Q ‚Üë definitely, P effect ambiguous
  • If both shift left: Q ‚Üì definitely, P effect ambiguous
  • If shifts in opposite directions: Need to know relative magnitudes

Rule: One effect is always ambiguous when both curves shift!

5. Consumer and Producer Surplus

Measures of welfare (benefit) that market participants receive.

Consumer and Producer Surplus

Welfare Measures

Consumer Surplus (CS): Difference between willingness to pay and actual price paid

$$CS = \int_0^{Q*} P_d(Q) \, dQ - P* \times Q*$$

Producer Surplus (PS): Difference between price received and willingness to supply

$$PS = P* \times Q* - \int_0^{Q*} P_s(Q) \, dQ$$

Total Surplus:

$$TS = CS + PS$$

Graphical Interpretation

  • Consumer Surplus: Area between demand curve and price (triangle above P*)
  • Producer Surplus: Area between price and supply curve (triangle below P*)
  • Total Surplus: Sum of both triangles
  • Deadweight Loss: Reduction in total surplus due to market distortions

Exercise 1: News Story Analysis

Task: Illustrate the following news stories using supply and demand graphs.

1a
Norway Oil Strike
"Norway's Equinor is temporarily shutting down three oil and gas fields after workers went on strike, intensifying regional supply troubles and pushing European gas prices to a four-month high."
Answer & Explanation

Analysis: The shutdown reduces supply. The supply curve shifts left, driving quantity down and prices up.

Oil Market: Supply Shock from Strike

Economic Reasoning:

  1. Strike reduces production capacity ‚Üí Supply decreases
  2. Supply curve shifts from S‚ÇÄ to S‚ÇÅ (leftward)
  3. New equilibrium: Lower quantity (Q‚ÇÄ ‚Üí Q‚ÇÅ), Higher price (P‚ÇÄ ‚Üí P‚ÇÅ)
  4. This creates a supply shortage at the original price
Short-run vs. Long-run

Short run: Supply shock causes large price increase (supply inelastic)

Long run: Other producers can increase output, substitutes emerge ‚Üí Price effect moderates

1b
Car Dealer Market - Pandemic Effects
"Fewer choices and higher prices" - pandemic shut down production for two months, but buyers came back sooner than expected.
Answer & Explanation

Analysis: This is a double shift scenario - both supply and demand change.

Car Market: Supply Decrease + Demand Increase

Economic Reasoning:

  • Supply side: Production shutdown ‚Üí Supply decreases (S‚ÇÄ ‚Üí S‚ÇÅ)
  • Demand side: Buyers return quickly ‚Üí Demand increases (D‚ÇÄ ‚Üí D‚ÇÅ)
  • Price effect: UNAMBIGUOUS INCREASE (both shifts push prices up)
  • Quantity effect: AMBIGUOUS (depends on relative magnitude of shifts)
  • Article states "fewer choices" ‚Üí Supply effect dominates, so Q‚ÇÅ < Q‚ÇÄ
Double Shift Rule

When supply decreases AND demand increases: Price ALWAYS increases, but quantity change depends on which shift is larger.

Exercise 2: Ice Cream Market Analysis

Scenario: Consider a market for ice cream in Paris. Analyze how various events affect equilibrium.

2a
Price of milk rises (Milk is a key input in ice cream production)
Answer & Explanation

Assumption: Milk is a key input in ice cream production.

Ice Cream Market: Input Price Increase

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Milk price ‚Üë ‚Üí Production costs ‚Üë
  2. Higher costs ‚Üí Firms willing to supply less at each price
  3. Supply curve shifts LEFT (S‚ÇÄ ‚Üí S‚ÇÅ)
  4. Demand curve UNCHANGED
  5. New equilibrium: P‚ÇÄ ‚Üí P‚ÇÅ (‚Üë), Q‚ÇÄ ‚Üí Q‚ÇÅ (‚Üì)

Numerical Example

If milk price increases by 20% and milk represents 30% of production costs:

Cost increase = 20% √ó 30% = 6%

This shifts supply curve up/left by ~6%

2c
Consumer incomes rise and ice cream is a normal good
Answer & Explanation

Key concept: Normal good = demand increases when income increases.

Ice Cream Market: Income Increase (Normal Good)

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Consumer income ‚Üë
  2. For normal goods: Income ‚Üë ‚Üí Demand ‚Üë
  3. Demand curve shifts RIGHT (D‚ÇÄ ‚Üí D‚ÇÉ)
  4. Supply curve UNCHANGED
  5. New equilibrium: P‚ÇÄ ‚Üí P‚ÇÉ (‚Üë), Q‚ÇÄ ‚Üí Q‚ÇÉ (‚Üë)
Normal vs. Inferior Goods
Good Type Income ‚Üë Demand Effect Examples
Normal Increases Demand ‚Üë Restaurants, travel, quality brands
Inferior Increases Demand ‚Üì Ramen noodles, bus tickets, fast food
2d
Summer suddenly turns very hot
Answer & Explanation

Key concept: Short-run supply inelasticity creates shortages.

Ice Cream Market: Sudden Demand Shock

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Temperature ‚Üë ‚Üí Demand for ice cream ‚Üë
  2. Demand curve shifts RIGHT (D‚ÇÄ ‚Üí D‚ÇÉ)
  3. Key assumption: Supply cannot adjust immediately (short run)
  4. If supply is perfectly inelastic (vertical): Q stays at Q‚ÇÄ ‚Üí Creates SHORTAGE
  5. Shortage = distance between Q‚ÇÄ and Qd at price P‚ÇÄ
  6. Price will rise to P‚ÇÉ to clear the market
Time Horizon Matters

Immediate (very short run):

  • Supply fixed (existing inventory)
  • Large price increase
  • Possible shortages

Short run (weeks):

  • Can increase production hours
  • Use existing capacity more intensively
  • Price still elevated but moderating

Long run (months/years):

  • Build new factories
  • New firms enter market
  • Price returns closer to original level
2e
Government sets maximum price during hot summer
Answer & Explanation

Key concept: Price ceilings create persistent shortages when set below equilibrium.

Price Ceiling During Hot Summer

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Hot summer: Demand increases D‚ÇÄ ‚Üí D‚ÇÉ
  2. Without price ceiling: Equilibrium would be (Q‚ÇÉ, P‚ÇÉ)
  3. Government sets maximum price at P* (below P‚ÇÉ)
  4. At price P*: Consumers want Qd, but producers only supply Qs
  5. Result: Persistent shortage = Qd - Qs

Problems with Price Ceilings

  • Shortage: Demand exceeds supply at the capped price
  • Rationing: Not everyone who wants ice cream can get it
    • First-come-first-served (long lines)
    • Favoritism by sellers
    • Black markets emerge
  • Quality degradation: Firms cut costs by reducing quality (can't compete on price)
  • Deadweight loss: Mutually beneficial trades don't occur
  • Search costs: Consumers waste time searching for the good

Historical Examples

  • Rent control: NYC, SF, Paris ‚Üí Housing shortages, deteriorating quality
  • Gas price controls (1970s): US ‚Üí Long lines at gas stations
  • Venezuela price controls: Food shortages, black markets
  • Ticket scalping laws: Concert tickets ‚Üí Underground markets
