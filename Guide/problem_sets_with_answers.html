Exercise 1: News Stories Analysis

Task: Illustrate the following news stories using supply and demand graphs.

1a. Norway Oil Strike

"Norway's Equinor is temporarily shutting down three oil and gas fields after workers went on strike... pushing European gas prices to a four-month high."

Answer:

Analysis: The shutdown means less supply. The supply curve shifts left, driving quantity down and prices up.

Oil Market: Supply Shock from Strike

Economic Reasoning:

  • Strike reduces production capacity → Supply decreases
  • Supply curve shifts from S₀ to S₁ (leftward)
  • New equilibrium: Lower quantity (Q₀ → Q₁), Higher price (P₀ → P₁)
  • This creates a supply shortage at the original price

1b. Car Dealer Market - Pandemic Effects

"Fewer choices and higher prices" - pandemic shut down production for two months, but buyers came back sooner than expected.

Answer:

Analysis: This is a double shift scenario - both supply and demand change.

Car Market: Supply Decrease + Demand Increase

Economic Reasoning:

  • Supply side: Production shutdown → Supply decreases (S₀ → S₁)
  • Demand side: Buyers return quickly → Demand increases (D₀ → D₁)
  • Price effect: UNAMBIGUOUS INCREASE (both shifts push prices up)
  • Quantity effect: AMBIGUOUS (depends on relative magnitude of shifts)
  • Article states "fewer choices" → Supply effect dominates, so Q₁ < Q₀
Key Insight

When supply decreases AND demand increases: Price ALWAYS increases, but quantity change depends on which shift is larger.

Exercise 2: Ice Cream Market Analysis

Task: Illustrate and explain how events affect equilibrium price and quantity of ice creams.

2a. Price of milk rises

Answer:

Assumption: Milk is a key input in ice cream production.

Ice Cream Market: Input Price Increase

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Milk price ↑ → Production costs ↑
  2. Higher costs → Firms willing to supply less at each price
  3. Supply curve shifts LEFT (S₀ → S₁)
  4. Demand curve UNCHANGED
  5. New equilibrium: P₀ → P₁ (↑), Q₀ → Q₁ (↓)

2b. Medical study shows chocolate bars are good for health

Answer:

Assumption: Ice cream and chocolate bars are substitutes.

Ice Cream Market: Substitute Good Becomes More Attractive

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Chocolate bars perceived as healthier → More attractive to consumers
  2. Some consumers switch from ice cream to chocolate bars
  3. Demand for ice cream DECREASES
  4. Demand curve shifts LEFT (D₀ → D₁)
  5. Supply curve UNCHANGED
  6. New equilibrium: P₀ → P₂ (↓), Q₀ → Q₂ (↓)

2c. Consumer incomes rise and ice cream is a normal good

Answer:

Key concept: Normal good = demand increases when income increases.

Ice Cream Market: Income Increase (Normal Good)

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Consumer income ↑
  2. For normal goods: Income ↑ → Demand ↑
  3. Demand curve shifts RIGHT (D₀ → D₃)
  4. Supply curve UNCHANGED
  5. New equilibrium: P₀ → P₃ (↑), Q₀ → Q₃ (↑)
Normal vs. Inferior Goods
  • Normal good: Income ↑ → Demand ↑ (most goods)
  • Inferior good: Income ↑ → Demand ↓ (e.g., ramen noodles, bus tickets)

2d. Summer suddenly turns very hot

Answer:

Key concept: Short-run supply inelasticity creates shortages.

Ice Cream Market: Sudden Demand Shock with Inelastic Supply

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Temperature ↑ → Demand for ice cream ↑
  2. Demand curve shifts RIGHT (D₀ → D₃)
  3. Key assumption: Supply cannot adjust immediately (short run)
  4. Supply remains at Q₀ in short run → Creates SHORTAGE
  5. Shortage = distance between Q₀ and Q₃ at price P₀
Short Run vs Long Run

Short run: Supply is fixed (can't add more production capacity) → Shortage occurs

Long run: Firms can build more factories, hire workers → Supply shifts right to meet demand

2e. Back to scratch. A newly elected government believes ice cream has gotten too costly. They decide to implement a maximum price. What happens if this price is set above equilibrium (3 points)? What happens if the price is set below equilibrium (3 points)?

Answer:

Case 1: Maximum Price Set ABOVE Equilibrium

If the maximum price is set above the equilibrium price, there is no effect. If suppliers choose to supply at that price, supply will exceed demand and this excess will lead to a drop in price, until the price reaches equilibrium price.

Non-Binding Price Ceiling

A price ceiling set above equilibrium is called "non-binding" because it doesn't constrain market behavior. The market naturally settles at the lower equilibrium price, so the ceiling has no practical effect.

Case 2: Maximum Price Set BELOW Equilibrium

If the maximum price is set below the equilibrium price, this means that suppliers will be willing to supply less than what consumers will be willing to demand (at that price). Scarcity will result from this. If the market was totally free, the price would have gone up – but it can't since the government implemented this maximum price.

Ice Cream Market: Price Ceiling Effects

Analysis of binding price ceiling (below equilibrium):

  1. Government sets maximum price P_max below equilibrium P*
  2. At P_max: Quantity demanded (Q_D) exceeds quantity supplied (Q_S)
  3. Result: Shortage = Q_D - Q_S
  4. Price cannot rise to eliminate shortage (it's illegal)
  5. Non-price rationing mechanisms emerge (queuing, favoritism, black markets)
Problems with Binding Price Ceilings
  • Persistent shortage: Demand exceeds supply at the capped price
  • Rationing required: Not everyone who wants ice cream can get it
  • Quality degradation: Firms may cut costs by reducing quality
  • Black markets: People may pay above the legal price illegally
  • Reduced investment: Lower prices discourage suppliers from expanding capacity

2f. Why would you criticize the measure described in (e) from a microeconomic standpoint? Explain in words (2 points) and illustrate with a graph (2 points).

Answer:

By setting a maximum price, the government is artificially limiting the amount of ice cream that will get traded on this market. Whereas this amount could have reached Q*, it is limited to Q_S because of this maximum price. The measure as a result is creating a deadweight loss (shown in yellow in the drawing) that society as a whole suffers from. This is why one could criticize this measure.

Deadweight Loss from Price Ceiling

Microeconomic Critique:

  1. Allocative inefficiency: Resources are not allocated to their highest-value use
  2. Deadweight loss: The yellow triangle represents transactions that would have benefited both buyers and sellers but don't occur
  3. Lost surplus: Total surplus (consumer + producer) is reduced compared to free market equilibrium
  4. Quantity traded falls: From Q* to Q_S, reducing overall market activity
Understanding Deadweight Loss

Deadweight loss represents the value of mutually beneficial transactions that don't occur because of the price ceiling:

  • Between Q_S and Q*, there are consumers willing to pay more than suppliers' costs
  • These trades would create value for both parties
  • The price ceiling prevents these trades from happening
  • Society as a whole is worse off by the area of the deadweight loss triangle

Who wins and who loses?

  • Winners: Consumers lucky enough to buy at the low price
  • Losers: Producers (lower price and quantity), consumers who can't buy due to shortage, society overall (deadweight loss)

Problem Set 3: International Trade & Industry Cost Curves

Key Concepts for This Problem Set

  • Gains from trade: How international trade affects surpluses
  • Industry cost curves: Merit-order dispatch and price determination

Main Event: International Trade Analysis

Scenario: A local economy with no ties to the rest of the world produces shoes. Then it opens to international trade.

1. Identify consumer and producer surplus in autarky

Answer:
Shoe Market in Autarky (No Trade)

Surplus Definitions:

  • Consumer Surplus (CS): Area A - represents benefit consumers get by paying less than their willingness to pay
  • Producer Surplus (PS): Area B+C - represents benefit producers get by receiving more than their cost
  • Total Surplus: CS + PS = A + B + C
Consumer Surplus = ½ × (P_max - P_A) × Q_A
Producer Surplus = ½ × (P_A - P_min) × Q_A

2. When the country opens to trade with cheaper foreign shoes

Answer:
Shoe Market After Opening to Trade

Analysis:

  • World price (P_W) < Autarky price (P_A): Foreign shoes are cheaper
  • At P_W:
    • Domestic quantity demanded: Q_d (increases from Q_A)
    • Domestic quantity supplied: Q_s (decreases from Q_A)
    • Imports = Q_d - Q_s (shown in orange)

3. Is trade good or bad for the economy? (Hint: Think in terms of surplus)

Answer:

Trade is GOOD for overall welfare.

Gains from Trade Analysis

Surplus Changes:

  • Consumer Surplus: A → A + B + D + E (INCREASES)
  • Producer Surplus: B + C → C (DECREASES)
  • Total Surplus: A + B + C → A + B + C + D + E
  • Net Gain from Trade: D + E (deadweight loss eliminated)
Key Insight: Trade Creates Winners and Losers
  • Winners: Consumers gain B + D + E
  • Losers: Domestic producers lose B
  • Net effect: Gains (D + E) > Losses, so trade increases total welfare
  • In principle, winners could compensate losers and still be better off

4. Why is trade hard to sell politically?

Answer:

The Political Economy Problem:

  • Gains are diffuse: Millions of consumers each gain a small amount (D + E distributed across many people)
  • Losses are concentrated: Small number of domestic producers lose B
  • Collective action problem:
    • Producers: Few in number, large losses each → Easy to organize lobbying
    • Consumers: Many, small gains each → Hard to organize politically
  • Visibility: Job losses are visible and newsworthy; cheaper prices are taken for granted
Example: US Steel Tariffs

When the US imposes steel tariffs:

  • ~140,000 steel workers benefit (visible, concentrated)
  • ~320 million consumers pay slightly higher prices for cars, appliances, etc. (invisible, diffuse)
  • Steel workers lobby effectively; consumers don't organize
  • Result: Politically popular despite reducing total welfare

5. How to make trade easier to sell politically?

Answer:

Policy Options Discussed:

  1. Tariffs (gradual opening):
    • Temporary tariff that decreases over time
    • Gives domestic industry time to adjust
    • Still captures most gains from trade eventually
  2. Improve competitiveness:
    • Reduce costs (e.g., lower taxes, energy costs)
    • Increase productivity (better technology, training)
    • Shifts domestic supply curve right
  3. Improve branding/quality:
    • Differentiate from foreign goods
    • Shift to higher-quality segments
    • Example: Italian shoes vs. Chinese shoes
  4. Transition assistance:
    • Use tariff revenue or tax revenue to help displaced workers
    • Retraining programs
    • Unemployment insurance
Exercise 3: Review - Labor Markets

Context: Consider a labor (job) market.

a) Who supplies what on this market? Who demands what? (2 points)

Answer:

On the labor market, individual workers supply labor. Firms demand labor.

Key Point

In labor markets, the roles are reversed from typical goods markets: workers are the suppliers (selling their labor), and firms are the demanders (buying labor services).

b) Represent in a diagram the wage w* and the number of workers n* in equilibrium. (2 points)

Answer:

n* is now the quantity, on the horizontal axis, and w* the price, on the vertical axis. The logic is really the same otherwise.

Labor Market Equilibrium

Key Elements:

  • Vertical axis: Wage (w) - the price of labor
  • Horizontal axis: Number of workers (n) - the quantity of labor
  • Supply curve: Upward sloping - higher wages attract more workers
  • Demand curve: Downward sloping - higher wages reduce firms' demand for workers
  • Equilibrium: Where supply meets demand → (n*, w*)

c) What happens to w* and to n* in a booming economy? Explain in words and represent graphically in the same diagram. (2 points)

Answer:

In a booming economy, firms will need more workers to tackle the increasing demand on their respective markets. As a result, their demand for workers will increase. This means that the demand curve will shift to the right.

At the new point of equilibrium, the equilibrium wage and the equilibrium number of people will both increase.

Labor Market: Booming Economy Effect (Demand Shift)

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Booming economy → Firms' product markets grow
  2. Increased production needs → Higher demand for workers
  3. Labor demand curve shifts RIGHT (D₀ → D₁)
  4. Labor supply curve UNCHANGED
  5. New equilibrium: w* increases, n* increases
Economic Intuition

When the economy booms, firms compete more intensely for workers, driving up wages and employment simultaneously.

d) What effects will the phenomena you described in (c) have on potential workers? Explain in words and represent graphically in the same diagram. (2 points)

Answer:

Some people are not on the job market – reasons may vary, but most often because the labor market is not attractive enough to them. The booming economy, improving prospects on the job market and higher wages will lead them to enter the job market, thereby increasing the supply of work.

The supply curve will therefore shift to the right, increasing the equilibrium number of people working but lowering the equilibrium wage.

Labor Market: Full Effect (Demand + Supply Shifts)

Complete analysis:

  1. First effect (from part c): Demand shifts right → ↑w*, ↑n*
  2. Second effect: Higher wages attract non-participants into labor market
  3. Labor supply curve shifts RIGHT (S₀ → S₁)
  4. Final equilibrium:
    • n* increases (unambiguous - both shifts increase quantity)
    • w* effect is ambiguous (demand shift pushes up, supply shift pushes down)
    • In this case, supply shift partially offsets the wage increase
Key Insight: Double Shift Analysis

When both demand and supply shift right:

  • Quantity (n*) ALWAYS increases
  • Price (w*) effect depends on relative magnitude of shifts
  • Here, the supply response moderates (but doesn't eliminate) the wage increase

Real-world example: During economic expansions, labor force participation rates often increase as discouraged workers, retirees, students, and caregivers enter or re-enter the job market attracted by better opportunities.

Exercise 4: Industry Cost Curve (McKinsey Article)

Context: Paper industry with demand of 75,000 tonnes. Three firms (Alpha, Bravo, Romeo) have production units with different costs and capacities.

Firm Unit Cost per Tonne ($) Capacity (tonnes)
Alpha Alpha 1 700 40,000
Alpha Alpha 2 850 25,000
Bravo Bravo 1 750 25,000
Bravo Bravo 2 1200 100,000
Romeo Romeo 1 1000 10,000

1. Which units will produce? What is the market price?

Answer:

Merit-Order Dispatch Logic: Units produce in order of cost until demand is met.

Industry Cost Curve (Merit Order)

Production Order:

  1. Alpha 1: Cost $700, Capacity 40,000 → Cumulative: 40,000 tonnes
  2. Bravo 1: Cost $750, Capacity 25,000 → Cumulative: 65,000 tonnes
  3. Alpha 2: Cost $850, Capacity 25,000 → Cumulative: 90,000 tonnes

Since demand = 75,000 tonnes:

  • Alpha 1 produces full capacity: 40,000 tonnes
  • Bravo 1 produces full capacity: 25,000 tonnes
  • Alpha 2 produces partial: 10,000 out of 25,000 capacity
  • Market price = $1,000 (cost of next available entrant = Romeo 1)
Price-Setting Logic

In competitive markets, price = marginal cost of the most expensive unit needed to meet demand.

Here, Alpha 2 is the marginal unit. It could charge up to $1,000 (Romeo's cost) without Romeo undercutting it.

2. Show graphically the profits each unit makes

Answer:

Profit Calculation: Profit = (Price - Cost) × Quantity

Unit Quantity Cost Price Profit per tonne Total Profit
Alpha 1 40,000 $700 $1,000 $300 $12,000,000
Bravo 1 25,000 $750 $1,000 $250 $6,250,000
Alpha 2 10,000 $850 $1,000 $150 $1,500,000
Profit Rectangles on Cost Curve

3. What if Bravo increases capacity of Bravo 1?

Answer:

Strategic Problem for Bravo:

If Bravo 1 capacity increases from 25,000 to 35,000+ tonnes:

  • Total production: Alpha 1 (40,000) + Bravo 1 (35,000) = 75,000 tonnes
  • Alpha 2 no longer needed → Exits market
  • New market price = $850 (Alpha 2's cost)
Effect of Bravo Capacity Expansion

Impact on Bravo 1:

  • Before: 25,000 tonnes × ($1,000 - $750) = $6,250,000 profit
  • After: 35,000 tonnes × ($850 - $750) = $3,500,000 profit
  • Net effect: LOSS of $2,750,000!
Capacity Paradox

Bravo should NOT expand capacity because:

  • Expansion pushes out Alpha 2 (the marginal unit)
  • This lowers market price from $1,000 to $850
  • The price decrease hurts Bravo MORE than the volume increase helps
  • This is why firms sometimes deliberately limit capacity!

4. Should Alpha close Alpha 2?

Answer:

Strategic Analysis:

If Alpha closes Alpha 2:

  • Remaining capacity: Alpha 1 (40,000) + Bravo 1 (25,000) = 65,000 tonnes
  • Demand = 75,000 → Shortage of 10,000 tonnes
  • Romeo 1 must enter to fill gap
  • New market price = $1,200 (Bravo 2's cost)
Effect of Alpha 2 Closure

Impact on Alpha 1:

  • Before closure: 40,000 × ($1,000 - $700) = $12,000,000
  • After closure: 40,000 × ($1,200 - $700) = $20,000,000
  • Gain from higher prices: +$8,000,000

Impact on Alpha 2:

  • Before closure: 10,000 × ($1,000 - $850) = $1,500,000 profit
  • After closure: $0 (but also saves $850 × 10,000 = $8,500,000 in costs if assuming no production = no costs)

Net effect for Alpha:

  • Gain on Alpha 1: +$8,000,000
  • Loss on Alpha 2: -$1,500,000
  • Net gain: +$6,500,000
  • Conclusion: Alpha SHOULD close Alpha 2!
Capacity Withholding Strategy

Firms can increase profits by strategically withholding capacity to drive up market prices.

This is why:

  • OPEC limits oil production
  • Diamond companies control supply
  • Airlines retire older planes during downturns

BUT: This requires market power and coordination. In perfectly competitive markets, other firms would fill the gap.

Problem Set 4: Price Elasticity & Strategic Pricing

Key Concepts for This Problem Set
  • Price elasticity of demand: How quantity demanded responds to price changes
  • Short run vs. long run elasticity: Time horizon affects flexibility
  • Dynamic pricing: Apple's iPhone pricing strategy
  • Price discrimination: Charging different prices to different customer segments
Warm-up: Price Elasticity

1. Which demand curve is more elastic?

Answer:
Elastic vs. Inelastic Demand

Key Insight:

  • Flatter curve (dd): MORE elastic - quantity changes a lot when price changes
  • Steeper curve (DD): LESS elastic - quantity doesn't change much when price changes
  • A drop from P₀ to P₁ causes larger quantity increase for dd than DD
Price Elasticity of Demand = (% Change in Quantity) / (% Change in Price)

|Elasticity| > 1: Elastic (responsive to price)
|Elasticity| < 1: Inelastic (not responsive to price)
|Elasticity| = 1: Unit elastic

2. Electricity demand: Which is short-run vs. long-run?

Answer:

dd (flatter) = Long-run demand

DD (steeper) = Short-run demand

Electricity Demand: Short Run vs. Long Run

Economic Reasoning:

  • Short run (DD - inelastic):
    • Capital is FIXED (can't change infrastructure)
    • If price increases, households/firms consume almost same amount
    • Can't easily change heating systems, industrial processes, etc.
    • Electricity is a necessity with few immediate substitutes
  • Long run (dd - elastic):
    • Capital is VARIABLE (can invest in energy-efficient appliances)
    • Can install solar panels, better insulation, etc.
    • Firms can adapt production processes
    • More time to find substitutes and adjust behavior
Time Horizon and Elasticity

General principle: Demand becomes MORE elastic over longer time periods because:

  1. More time to find substitutes
  2. More time to adjust capital stock (equipment, infrastructure)
  3. More time for new technologies to emerge
  4. More time for habits to change
Main Event: Apple's iPhone Pricing Strategy

Context: Apple launched the iPhone at $600, then quickly dropped it to $400, angering early adopters.

1. How would you justify the price cut from a microeconomic standpoint?

Answer:

Dynamic Pricing & Product Lifecycle Strategy:

Apple's Dynamic Pricing Strategy

Three Economic Justifications:

1. Price Discrimination (Third-Degree)

  • Early adopters: High willingness to pay, INELASTIC demand
    • Tech enthusiasts who must have new gadgets
    • Status seekers
    • Less price-sensitive
  • Mass market: Lower willingness to pay, ELASTIC demand
    • Wait for reviews and lower prices
    • More price-sensitive
    • Larger volume but lower margins
  • By launching at $600, Apple captures consumer surplus from early adopters
  • By dropping to $400, Apple captures mass market (Job's goal: "make product affordable for holidays")

2. Product Lifecycle Model

  • Introduction phase ($600): Low competition, "breakthrough product," inelastic demand
  • Growth phase ($400): Competition increases, demand becomes elastic, need volume
  • This is textbook dynamic pricing strategy

3. Behavioral Economics / Anchoring

  • By showing $600 first, Apple creates an anchor
  • $400 now seems like a "great deal" (33% discount!)
  • Without the anchor, consumers wouldn't know if $400 is expensive or cheap
  • Apple essentially ran a pricing experiment
Profit Maximization Logic:

Phase 1 (High price): π₁ = (P₁ - MC) × Q₁ = ($600 - MC) × Q_early_adopters

Phase 2 (Low price): π₂ = (P₂ - MC) × Q₂ = ($400 - MC) × Q_mass_market

Total profit = π₁ + π₂ > profit from single price strategy

2. Why would you criticize it from a microeconomic standpoint?

Answer:

Problem: Affecting Future Price Expectations

Impact on Future Demand

Economic Critiques:

1. Demand is Driven by Price Expectations

  • Cutting prices so quickly sends signal: "Wait, prices will drop"
  • Future early adopters will delay purchases
  • This flattens the demand curve (makes it more elastic)
  • Reduces ability to price discriminate in future

2. Brand Damage / Trust Issues

  • Early adopters feel "ripped off"
  • Reduces loyalty and brand equity
  • May not buy future iPhones immediately (→ wait for price drop)

3. Credibility Problem

  • Apple's implicit promise: "Premium price = premium product"
  • Quick price drop suggests: "We overpriced it" or "Demand was weak"
  • Undermines premium positioning
Trade-off: Short-term Profit vs. Long-term Strategy

Short-term gain: Capture more consumer surplus through price discrimination

Long-term risk: Train customers to wait for price drops, reducing future pricing power

What Apple did right: Offered $100 store credit to early adopters to mitigate anger

Evidence from iPhone History:

  • iPhone (2007): $600 → $400 after 2 months
  • iPhone 3G onward: More gradual price reductions
  • Current strategy: Keep flagship price stable, introduce cheaper models (SE, older generations)
  • Suggests Apple learned from the backlash

Question 2: Is Uber Representative of the Sharing Economy?

2a. What is the sharing economy? Does Uber fit?

Answer:

Richard Waters' Definition (FT article):

"It is a disruptive economic form that unleashes new sources of supply. Whether this involves underused assets (private cars, spare bedrooms) or labour (people willing to fill in a few hours as taxi drivers, personal shoppers or couriers), it taps resources that could not be effectively organised before."

Does Uber Fit? YES - on multiple dimensions:

1. Underused Assets (Private Cars)

  • Average car sits idle 95% of the time
  • UberX allows car owners to monetize idle capacity
  • This is supply side of sharing economy

2. Flexible Labour

  • Part-time drivers: Students, retirees, people with day jobs
  • "Fill in a few hours" → Can drive during peak hours only
  • Reduces labor market friction (match people with flexible supply to variable demand)

3. Platform Coordination

  • Before Uber: Couldn't organize thousands of part-time drivers
  • GPS + smartphones + algorithms made coordination possible
  • Technology unlocked latent supply
Sharing Economy: Unlocking Latent Supply

Graph Explanation:

  • Pre-Uber: Only professional taxis (red supply curve) - high cost, limited supply
  • Post-Uber: Adds part-time drivers with spare cars (blue supply curve shifts right)
  • Result: Lower prices (P₁ to P₂), higher quantity (Q₁ to Q₂)
  • Deadweight loss eliminated (gains from trade in green)

2b. Why do microeconomists agree Uber represents sharing economy?

Answer:

Three Microeconomic Arguments:

1. Marginal Cost ≈ $0

  • Cost to Uber of one additional ride ≈ $0 (just software matching)
  • This is characteristic of platform businesses
  • Contrasts with traditional taxis (buying fleet, maintenance, dispatch center)
  • Enables extreme scalability
Traditional Taxi: MC = fuel + wear & tear + driver wage + dispatch overhead
Uber: MC ≈ $0 (driver pays fuel & car costs, Uber just matches)

2. Platform Model: Matching Supply and Demand

  • Uber is essentially a market-maker
  • Uses price signals (surge pricing) to equilibrate supply and demand
  • Quote from case: "What you are aiming for is the equilibrium of supply and demand. A perfect day is when you set an all-time record for trips per hour with zero surges."
  • This is textbook microeconomics!
Uber's Dynamic Equilibrium

Normal vs. Peak Times:

  • Normal time: Supply and demand meet at P₀, Q₀ (no surge)
  • Peak time: Demand surges to D₁
    • Without surge: Shortage (Q₀ < Q₁)
    • With surge to P₁: Attracts more drivers, equilibrium at Q₁
    • Surge serves to clear the market

3. Efficiency of Market Design

  • Price discovery: Algorithm finds market-clearing price in real-time
  • No deadweight loss: Unlike regulated taxi markets with price ceilings
  • Resource allocation: Drivers go where demand is highest (follow price signals)
  • Information aggregation: Platform has perfect information about supply & demand
Why Microeconomists Love Uber

Uber is a real-world example of:

  1. Supply and demand equilibrium - surge pricing
  2. Price elasticity - different elasticities at different times
  3. Two-sided markets - need to balance riders and drivers
  4. Information economics - ratings solve asymmetric information
  5. Market efficiency - eliminates deadweight loss from regulation
  6. Marginal cost pricing - MC ≈ 0, can scale infinitely

Counterarguments (Uber as "NOT" sharing economy):

  • Full-time drivers: Many drivers are now full-time (not "sharing" idle time)
  • Commercial purpose: Cars bought specifically for Uber (not "underused assets")
  • Platform extraction: Uber takes 25-30% commission (not peer-to-peer sharing)
  • Labor exploitation concerns: Drivers classified as contractors, not employees
