üìö Key Terms Glossary

Use this glossary to quickly review the main concepts from all sessions.

Consumer Surplus

Definition: The total value consumers get *above* what they paid for a product. It's the area below the demand curve but above the equilibrium price.

Significance: Represents the "profit" or extra value consumers receive from a free market transaction.

Deadweight Loss

Definition: Value that is destroyed and lost to everyone in the market, typically due to an intervention like a tax or tariff. Represented by "two black triangles" in the free trade model.

Demand Curve

Definition: A graph showing the relationship *only* between the price of a good and the quantity demanded, assuming all other drivers are equal.

Movement along curve: Caused *only* by a change in the good's own price.

Shift of curve: Caused by a change in substitutes, complements, income, tastes, or price expectations.

Economy

Definition: A system where people and organizations create, exchange, and distribute scarce resources under a set of rules.

Economies of Scale

Definition: The phenomenon where, as production (Q) increases, the Average Cost (AC) and Marginal Cost (MC) of production fall. This is due to learning, specialization, and spreading fixed costs.

Equilibrium (Market)

Definition: The point where the supply and demand curves intersect. This determines the natural market price (P*) and quantity (Q*) where the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded.

Globalization ("Elephant Chart")

Definition: A famous chart showing real income gains globally from 1988-2008. It shows that the biggest winners were the Asian middle class and the global top 1%, while the biggest (relative) losers were the lower/middle classes in rich Western countries.

Inelastic Demand

Definition: A steep demand curve where demand is *less reactive* to price changes. Consumers will buy the product even if the price goes up (e.g., medicine, addictive drugs).

Managerial Economics

Definition: Understanding the "mechanics of the car." It provides tools to analyze the microeconomic environment (individual markets, firms, and people) to make better business decisions.

Marginal Analysis

Definition: The core principle of firm decision-making. Firms assess the costs and benefits of the *next* action or unit.

Marginal Cost (MC)

Definition: The extra cost incurred from producing *one additional unit* of a good.

Marginal Revenue (MR)

Definition: The extra revenue earned from selling *one additional unit* of a good.

Market

Definition: A place or system where demand meets supply.

Merit Order (Supply Principle)

Definition: In a competitive market, the unit with the lowest production cost will produce first, then the next-cheapest, and so on, until demand is met. The market price is set by the *last and most expensive unit* needed.

Price Discrimination

Definition: A pricing strategy where a firm charges different prices to different consumers or groups to capture more consumer surplus (e.g., auctions, student discounts, geo-marketing).

Price Elasticity

Definition: A measure of how reactive demand is to a change in price. An "elastic" (flat) curve means demand is highly reactive. An "inelastic" (steep) curve means demand is not very reactive.

Price Floor (Price Minimum)

Definition: An intervention (like minimum wage) that sets a price *above* the equilibrium price. This always causes an *Excess* (or surplus) because supply will be greater than demand.

Price Ceiling (Price Maximum)

Definition: An intervention (like rent control) that sets a price *below* the equilibrium price. This always causes a *Shortage* because demand will be greater than supply.

Producer Surplus

Definition: The total profit producers make *above* their cost to produce. It's the area above the supply curve but below the equilibrium price.

Profit Maximization Rule

Definition: The rule that rational firms follow. They continue to produce as long as Marginal Revenue > Marginal Cost. They stop producing where MR = MC, as this is the point where total profit is maximized.

Sharing Economy

Definition: A disruptive model that "unleashes new sources of supply" by tapping into *underused assets* (like spare rooms via Airbnb) or *labor* (like drivers via Uber) using online platforms.

Drivers: Powered by near-zero marginal costs of digitalization, platforms (coordination), and connectivity (utilizing idle capacity).

Supply Curve

Definition: A graph showing the relationship *only* between the price of a good and the quantity supplied, assuming all other drivers are equal.

Movement along curve: Caused *only* by a change in the good's own price.

Shift of curve: Caused by a change in the number of firms, cost of inputs, or productivity.

üõí What Drives Demand?

The demand function relates the demand for a good to all its determinants.

Demand Function (Drivers) \[Demand = f(p, s, c, I, t, e)\]
  • p (Price of the good): Greater price ‚Üí lower demand ("law of demand").
  • s (Price of substitutes): Cheaper alternatives ‚Üí lower demand.
  • c (Price of complements): Higher price of a complement (gas for a car) ‚Üí lower demand.
  • I (Income): Can the customer afford it?
  • t (Preferences and tastes): Does the customer want it?
  • e (Price expectations): If price is expected to rise, demand might increase now.

The Demand Curve

The demand curve is a simpler graph showing the relationship *only* between demand and price, assuming all other drivers (s, c, I, t, e) are held equal.

Linear Demand Curve \[P = a - bQ\]

Movement vs. Shift

  • A movement *along* the curve only happens when the good's own price changes.
  • A *shift* of the entire curve happens when one of the other drivers changes (e.g., price of a substitute, income, tastes).

Example: Shifts in Demand

  • Shift Right (Increase): Price of substitutes $\uparrow$, price of complements $\downarrow$, tastes improve, or price expected to rise.
  • Shift Left (Decrease): Price of substitutes $\downarrow$, price of complements $\uparrow$, tastes decline, or price expected to fall.

üè≠ What Drives Supply?

The supply function relates the supply of a good to its determinants.

Supply Function (Drivers) \[Supply = f(p, \#, i, \Delta)\]
  • p (Price of the good): Higher price ‚Üí more supply ("law of supply").
  • # (Number of firms): More firms ‚Üí more supply.
  • i (Cost of inputs): Higher cost of labor/materials ‚Üí less supply.
  • Œî (Productivity changes): Better technology/productivity ‚Üí more supply.

The Supply Curve

The supply curve shows the relationship *only* between supply and price, assuming all other drivers (#, i, Œî) are held equal.

Linear Supply Curve \[P = c + dQ\]

Movement vs. Shift

  • A movement *along* the curve only happens when the good's own price changes.
  • A *shift* of the entire curve happens when one of the other drivers changes (e.g., number of firms, input costs).

‚öñÔ∏è Market Equilibrium & Interventions

The market naturally finds an equilibrium price (P*) and quantity (Q*) where the supply and demand curves intersect.

Gains from Trade

  • Consumer Surplus: The area *below* the demand curve but *above* the equilibrium price (P*). This is the value consumers get *above* what they paid.
  • Producer Surplus: The area *above* the supply curve but *below* the equilibrium price (P*). This is the profit producers make *above* their cost to produce.

Economists favor free markets because they maximize the total gains from trade (the sum of consumer and producer surplus).

Market Interventions

Sometimes, an actor (like the government) intervenes in the market.

Price Minimum (Pmin)

A price (like minimum wage) set above the equilibrium P*.

Result: Causes an Excess (Surplus), because supply (Qs) will be greater than demand (Qd).

Price Maximum (Pmax)

A price (like rent control) set below the equilibrium P*.

Result: Causes a Shortage, because demand (Qd) will be greater than supply (Qs).

The "Merit Order" Supply Principle

In a competitive market, the unit with the lowest production cost produces first, up to its capacity. The next-cheapest produces next, and so on, until total demand is met.

Price Setting: The market price is set by the *last* and *most expensive* unit needed to satisfy total demand.

Paper Industry Example

Total demand is 75,000 tons.

  1. Alpha 1 (Cost 700) produces 40,000 tons.
  2. Bravo 1 (Cost 750) produces 25,000 tons.
  3. Alpha 2 (Cost 850) produces the final 10,000 tons.

Result: The market price is 850 (the cost of Alpha 2), and *all producers* (Alpha 1, Bravo 1, Alpha 2) get paid this price.

The EU Electricity Problem

The EU uses this "merit order" system. The supply stack (cheapest to costliest) is: 1. Renewables, 2. Nuclear, 3. Coal, 4. Natural Gas, 5. Oil.

The Problem: The price is often set by the most expensive unit needed: natural gas. This leads to very high prices for everyone and makes the EU less competitive.

üí∞ Session 4: Pricing Strategies

Understanding how firms can use microeconomic principles to develop pricing strategies.

Strategy 1: Price Discrimination

The Goal: Extracting Consumer Surplus

Consumer Surplus is the money "left on the table" ‚Äì the amount consumers were *willing* to spend but didn't have to, because the market price was lower than their valuation.

The goal of price discrimination is for the firm to capture a greater chunk of this surplus by discriminating between different types of consumers.

Examples:

  • Auctions: Letting customers "name their price" (e.g., bidding for upgrades).
  • Grouping: Charging flat rates (amusement parks) or "ladies' night" specials.
  • Geo-marketing: Charging different prices based on location.

Strategy 2: Price Elasticity

The Concept: Harnessing Reactions to Price

A simple cost-plus markup (e.g., cost + 20%) is a *poor* strategy because it ignores the consumer.

A better strategy focuses on price elasticity, which measures how reactive demand is to a change in price.

  • Inelastic Demand (Steeper Curve): Demand is *less reactive* to price changes. Consumers will buy even if the price goes up.
  • Elastic Demand (Flatter Curve): Demand is *more reactive* to price changes. A small price drop can lead to a large increase in demand.

Promotions like "Buy 1 Get 1 Free" are designed to appeal to consumers with highly elastic demand.

Case Study: Dynamic Pricing (iPhone 2007)

This case shows how strategies change over a product's life.

  1. Introduction (Price Skimming): Apple launched the iPhone at $600. This targeted "early adopters" who have very inelastic demand and were willing to pay a premium.
  2. The Pivot (Targeting Elasticity): Just two months later, Apple cut the price to $400. This was a strategic pivot to target the "mainstream" market, which has a more elastic demand, for the holiday season.
  3. The Backlash: Early adopters were furious (feeling "used").
  4. The Resolution: Steve Jobs offered a $100 store credit to appease this loyal, inelastic group.

üè≠ Session 5: Cost and Supply - How Firms Behave

This session focuses on the costs that influence firm decisions, particularly marginal cost, and how it drives new business models like the sharing economy.

The Firm: Behavior and Decisions

We assume firms are rational actors aiming to maximize profits. This involves deciding *whether* to produce and *how much* to produce.

Marginal Analysis: The Core Principle

Firms make decisions "at the margin," assessing the costs and benefits of the *next* action or unit.

  • Marginal Revenue (MR): The extra revenue from selling one additional unit.
  • Marginal Cost (MC): The extra cost from producing one additional unit.
The Profit Maximization Rule

A firm continues to produce as long as MR > MC.

Total profit is maximized when:

\[MR = MC\]

Understanding Cost Curves

  • Economies of Scale: Initially, as production (Q) increases, Average Cost (AC) and Marginal Cost (MC) fall (due to learning, specialization, etc.).
  • Diseconomies of Scale: Eventually, as Q grows very large, AC and MC may rise (due to complexity, coordination issues).
  • Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The output level (Q) where Average Cost is minimized.

üí° The Significance of Near-Zero Marginal Cost (MC ‚âà 0)

What happens when the cost of producing one more unit approaches zero? This phenomenon drives many new business models.

Three Key Drivers of MC ‚âà 0:

  1. Digitalization: The marginal cost of copying digital goods (software, music) is virtually zero.
  2. Platforms: Drastically reduce the marginal cost of *coordination*‚Äîmatching buyers and sellers (e.g., Uber, Airbnb). They leverage *network effects*.
  3. Connectivity (IoT): Connecting physical assets (cars, homes) allows for hyper-efficient utilization, reducing the marginal cost of *idle capacity*.

Definition: A disruptive model that "unleashes new sources of supply" by tapping into underused assets (spare rooms, private cars) or labor (gig workers) that couldn't be efficiently organized before.

Economic Engine: Powered by the near-zero marginal costs of information, coordination, and utilizing idle capacity.

Expected Impacts:

  • Deflationary: Increases supply, putting downward pressure on prices.
  • Disruptive: Imposes rapid change on traditional industries.
  • Game-Changing: Organizes new areas of economic activity.

Uber exemplifies these near-zero marginal cost principles.

The Business Model & MC=0:

  • Uber doesn't own cars; it's a platform connecting riders and drivers (MC of adding a user is near zero).
  • It efficiently utilizes underused assets (drivers' personal cars) and labor.
  • Sophisticated algorithms manage supply and demand in real-time.

Surge Pricing

Uber's dynamic pricing mechanism increases fares when demand outstrips supply (e.g., rush hour).

Rationale: To incentivize more drivers (supply) to come online, balancing the market and reducing wait times.

Controversy: Accusations of price gouging, especially during emergencies.

Disruption & Challenges:

  • Competition: Directly competes with (and disrupts) the traditional, regulated taxi industry.
  • Regulatory Battles: Uber's strategy of "move fast and break things" led to constant legal fights globally.
  • Driver Status: The classification of drivers as *independent contractors* (not employees) is central to the model but faces constant legal challenges.

  • Given a demand curve $P = a - bQ$ and a constant $MC$, find the profit function: $\pi = (P \times Q) - (MC \times Q)$.

  • Perfect Competition: Understand that in a perfectly competitive market, price is driven down to marginal cost. The equilibrium price would be $P = MC$.

Question 1: Market Intervention

The government imposes "rent control," a law stating that apartments cannot be rented for more than $1,000 per month. The current market equilibrium rent is $1,500 per month.

What will be the result of this policy?

Solution: A Shortage

This is a Price Maximum (Price Ceiling) set *below* the equilibrium price.

  • At the lower $1,000 price, *more* people will want to rent (demand increases).
  • At the same time, *fewer* landlords will be willing to rent out their properties (supply decreases).

When demand is greater than supply, the result is a shortage of apartments.

Question 2: Supply & Demand Shifts

What happens to the equilibrium price and quantity of gasoline if a major new oil field is discovered (increasing productivity)?

Solution: Price Decreases, Quantity Increases

A new oil field is a change in one of the drivers of *supply* (specifically, productivity Œî).

  1. The supply curve shifts to the right (more supply at every price).
  2. The demand curve does not change.
  3. The new intersection point (equilibrium) will be at a lower price (P*) and a higher quantity (Q*).

Question 3: Firm Behavior

A coffee shop calculates that for its next cup of coffee, the marginal revenue (MR) is $3.00 and the marginal cost (MC) is $2.50.

To maximize profit, should the shop sell this next cup?

Solution: Yes, it should.

The Profit Maximization Rule states that a firm should continue to produce as long as MR > MC.

  • Here, MR ($3.00) > MC ($2.50).
  • This means that selling one more cup of coffee adds $0.50 to the shop's total profit.

The shop should continue producing until MR = MC.

Question 4: Pricing Strategy

Apple launches a new product at a very high price, targeting only the most enthusiastic fans. Six months later, it drops the price significantly to appeal to the general public.

What pricing strategies is Apple using?

Solution: Price Skimming & Elasticity

This is a form of dynamic pricing and price discrimination over time.

  1. Phase 1 (Price Skimming): The high launch price targets "early adopters," who have very inelastic demand (they are not price-sensitive). This captures their high consumer surplus.
  2. Phase 2 (Targeting Elasticity): The later price drop targets the "mainstream" market, which has a more elastic demand (they are more price-sensitive).
