1.2 Demand Side Drivers (4 Factors)

1
Price (P): As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (Law of Demand)
Movement ALONG the curve (IN the curve)
2
Price of Substitutes: Higher substitute prices ‚Üí Higher demand for your product
Example: Coffee prices rise ‚Üí Tea demand increases
Shifts curve RIGHT (movement OF the curve)
3
Price of Complements (strings attached): Higher complement prices ‚Üí Lower demand for your product
Example: Printer prices rise ‚Üí Ink demand decreases
Shifts curve LEFT
4
Price Expectations: Expected future price increases ‚Üí Current demand increases
Shifts curve RIGHT
Demand Function: D = f(P) ceteris paribus (everything else remains constant)
Linear Form: P = a - bQ
where a = intercept (max price), b = slope
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL Distinction:
‚Ä¢ Movement IN the curve (along): Price change only
‚Ä¢ Movement OF the curve (shift): Changes in other factors (substitutes, complements, expectations)

DO NOT confuse these!
üí° Curve Slope Matters!
‚Ä¢ Steeper curve: Less impact on demand from price changes (inelastic)
‚Ä¢ Flatter curve: Greater impact on demand from price changes (elastic)

1.3 Supply Side Drivers (3 Factors)

1
Number of Competitors/Firms: More firms ‚Üí More supply (shifts RIGHT)
2
Cost of Inputs (Production Costs): Higher input costs ‚Üí Lower supply (shifts LEFT)
Los costos de competencia afectan la oferta
3
Productivity Shocks/Technology: Technology improvements ‚Üí Higher supply (shifts RIGHT)
Supply Function: P = c + dQ
where c = minimum price to produce, d = slope

Key Insight:
‚Ä¢ Low price ‚Üí Producer willing and able to produce is LOW
‚Ä¢ High price ‚Üí Producer willing and able to produce is HIGH

1.4 Market Equilibrium - Where Demand Meets Supply

Equilibrium is where demand meets supply:

  • Q* = Equilibrium quantity (total amount of goods being traded at P*)
  • P* = Equilibrium price (where everyone is "happy")
  • Key Principle: At equilibrium, Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied
  • Economic Value: All opportunities in the market - shrinking or growing?

1.5 Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer Surplus (CS)

The aggregate sum of money that consumers were willing to pay but did not have to pay because market forces set prices at a lower level

  • Triangle area ABOVE P* and BELOW demand curve
  • Represents value captured by consumers (what they save)
  • Difference between willingness to pay and actual price paid
  • Example: If you were willing to pay P'=12 but only paid P*=10, you gained 2 in CS

Producer Surplus (PS) / Business Surplus

The difference between the price producers receive and their minimum acceptable price (marginal cost)

  • Triangle area BELOW P* and ABOVE supply curve
  • Represents value captured by producers/businesses (what they gain)
  • Also called "business surplus" or "profit of monopoly"
Total Economic Value (Gains from Trade) = Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus

Triangle Area Formula: ¬Ω √ó base √ó height
CS = ¬Ω √ó Q* √ó (Pmax - P*) [ABOVE P*]
PS = ¬Ω √ó Q* √ó (P* - Pmin) [BELOW P*]
Consumer and Producer Surplus
üí° Remember: Consumer surplus is ABOVE equilibrium price (what they save), Producer surplus is BELOW equilibrium price (what they gain above costs)

1.7 Government Intervention

Price Floor (Minimum Price - Pmin)

  • Set by government to protect producers/businesses
  • If Pmin > P* ‚Üí Creates EXCESS SUPPLY (surplus/unemployment)
  • Example: Minimum wage, agricultural price supports
  • Result: Qs > Qd ‚Üí Unemployment or waste
Price Floor Effect

Price Ceiling (Maximum Price - Pmax)

  • Set by government to protect consumers
  • If Pmax < P* ‚Üí Creates EXCESS DEMAND (shortage)
  • Example: Rent control
  • Result: Qd > Qs ‚Üí Shortages
Price Ceiling Effect

Tariffs and Trade Policy

Scenario: A country opens to trade, world price (Pw) < domestic price (PA)

  • Without tariff: Consumers benefit (more CS), domestic producers lose (less PS)
  • With tariff: Price rises from Pw to PT = Pw + T
  • Winners: Domestic producers (expand market share), Government (tax revenue)
  • Losers: Consumers (higher prices), Overall efficiency (deadweight loss)
  • Deadweight Loss: The two triangles - gains from trade that disappear
Tariff Effects on Trade
Why use tariffs despite deadweight loss?
  • Political pressure from domestic suppliers
  • Protect "strategic" industries (e.g., textiles, agriculture)
  • Short-term political gains vs. long-term economic efficiency
  • National security concerns
  • Premium brand effect: "Made in my country" - quality perception

Minimum Wage Analysis

  • If min wage < W*: No effect - firms raise wages to W* anyway
  • If min wage > W*: Creates unemployment
  • Labor demanded: Ld (lower)
  • Labor supplied: Ls (higher)
  • Unemployment: Ls - Ld
Minimum Wage Effects on Labor Market
‚ö†Ô∏è Productivity and Minimum Wage:
Marginal productivity defines wages in competitive markets. Minimum wage that exceeds productivity can lead to skill-based exclusion - some workers may be priced out of the market even though they want to work!
‚ö†Ô∏è Deadweight Loss: The loss in total surplus (consumer + producer surplus) due to market inefficiency caused by government intervention. It's value that COULD have been created but wasn't!

1.8 Elasticity - How Consumers React to Price Changes

What is Elasticity?

Elasticity measures how responsive quantity demanded is to price changes.

Key Question from notes: What does a drop in price trigger?

Elastic Demand (FLAT Curve - DD)

  • Large change in quantity for small price change
  • Many substitutes available
  • Non-essential items
  • Examples: Generic products, luxury items, pizza in Paris
  • Strategy: Lower price ‚Üí Increase revenue
  • From notes: "A flat curve will be more affected by a change in price"
  • Key insight: Increase in demand will be relatively higher than decrease in price = higher revenues

Inelastic Demand (STEEP Curve - dd)

  • Small change in quantity for large price change
  • Few substitutes available
  • Essential items
  • Examples: Insulin, gasoline (short run), addictive products
  • Strategy: Raise price ‚Üí Increase revenue
  • Less affected by price changes
Elastic vs. Inelastic Demand
üì± iPhone Example:
At launch: Inelastic (few substitutes, high willingness to pay, early adopters) ‚Üí Apple charges high price
Over time: More elastic (more competitors, more substitutes) ‚Üí Prices gradually decrease

Note: This explains Apple's famous price cut controversy - early buyers paid premium prices!
üí° Using Promotions to Test Elasticity (from class):
Promotions help businesses understand how elastic/inelastic their demand is to price changes. Watch how quantity changes when you change price!

Basic Profit Function (from notes):

Marginal Revenue (MR)

Definition from notes: "How much do I earn for the EXTRA unit"

MR = ŒîTR/ŒîQ (change in total revenue / change in quantity)

MR = New Revenue - Past Revenue

For linear demand P = a - bQ: MR = a - 2bQ

üí° Notice the "2b" - this is crucial! The slope of MR is twice as steep as demand. This is why MR curve is always below the demand curve for monopolists.

Marginal Cost (MC)

Definition from notes: "How much does it cost to produce the EXTRA unit"

MC = ŒîTotal Cost/ŒîQuantity

‚òÖ ASSUMPTION (from class): "Cost of producing first unit = the 1000th unit"

Note: This is a simplification! In reality, MC changes with economies of scale, learning by doing, and task specialization (see MES below)

Marginal Profit (MP)

MP = MR - MC

The profit generated by selling ONE extra unit

When MP = 0, profits are at maximum!

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

From notes: "MES - minimum efficiency scale defines the firm's optimal scale and size"

  • Econom√≠as de escala: As production increases, average costs decrease
  • Learning by doing: Workers become more efficient
  • Task specialization: Division of labor increases productivity
  • "Learning by doing and task specialization lead a firm to becomes efficient"
  • After MES, diseconomies of scale may occur (coordination costs rise)

2.2 The Golden Rule: MR = MC

üéØ Profit Maximization Rule

A firm maximizes profit where MR = MC

This is equivalent to:

  • Producing until marginal profit = 0
  • Finding where the derivative of the profit function = 0
  • The slope of the profit curve = 0 (at the peak!)
  • From notes: "We want to reach here" (at the top of the profit curve)

Why stop at MR = MC? (from class)

"MR = MC is when you stop, and when doing an extra unit gives you zero profit. At the HIGHEST level of profit"

  • If MR > MC: Producing one more unit adds to profit ‚Üí Keep producing!
  • If MR < MC: Producing one more unit reduces profit ‚Üí Stop producing!
  • If MR = MC: No additional profit from extra unit ‚Üí Optimal point! ‚úì
Profit Maximization: MR = MC (with Profit Curve)
üí° The Excel Way (from class):
Calculate MR and MC for each quantity, find where they're equal!
Marginal profit = MR - MC
When marginal profit = 0, total profit is at maximum!
